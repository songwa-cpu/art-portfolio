Project I · Real Gangstas Show Love

Unity through Adornment

My practice is inseparable from ritual — alongside my altar, guided by ancestral wisdom and spiritual communication. My visual language is inspired by Middle Eastern, Indian, and African maximalist design traditions, where color, pattern, and abundance are embraced rather than subdued. I am drawn to these aesthetics because they celebrate presence and excess as forms of power. In contrast, Western visual culture often favors restraint and minimalism — an approach I see as culturally limiting. My work intentionally resists this by asserting vibrancy as a political act and cultural affirmation. I'm so grateful for this opportunity to be the representation of unity that I wish I had as a youth.
Project III

Critical Transformation

My practice sits inside rupture — the moments when what no longer serves us has to break before something truer can take its place. This body of work is about that threshold: the discomfort of shedding old identities, inherited pain, and imposed narratives, and the fierce clarity that follows. These pieces aren't decorative — they're confrontations, with the self, with history, with what we've been taught to accept. To transform is not to soften. It is to break honestly, and rebuild with intention.
Project II

Revealing the Divine through Us All

My practice explores the idea that there is a sacred essence within every individual — a connection to something greater than ourselves. Through symbolic abstraction and spiritual expression, these works seek to remind viewers of their own divinity, consciousness, and interconnectedness with all life. The work encourages viewers to look inward, recognize their own sacred nature, and experience a deeper relationship with themselves, others, and the universe.
